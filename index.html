Improvement of an Existing Robotic Exoskeleton

In the final year of an engineering degree, students are required to pair up and complete a year long project to demonstrate the knowledge and skills gained during our time at uiversity. The project my partner and I chose was to improve upon an already existing rehabilitation exoskeleton device. After completing a comprehensive literature review, two key aspects were identified as being under developed in both the exoskeleton we had permission to work on and the general robotic exoskeleton field. One of these aspects was the addition of additional degrees of freedom to the exoskeleton to allow the device to follow a persons gait cycle more precisely. For me, this meant development of the exoskeletons back support as previously it only incorporated a single degree of freedom to allow a persons back to move up and down. After a significant amount of research into the motion of the back during gait and how these movements are affected in a stroke patient, three motions were identified as 'important to control'. However due to time and cost constraints, only two of these were designed for in this project (see section 3.1.1 for full report). The final design of the back support can be seen below implementing the two new degrees of freedom, as well as the original motion implemented. Autonomous SLAM Robot

Simultaneous Localisation and Mapping (SLAM) is a complex problem in which our robotic vehicle (shown below) is required to construct and update a map of an unknown environment while simultaneously keeping track of it's own location. For this project, we were supplied with a partially completed robot consisting of four motors attached to Mecanum wheels and an Arduino Mega attached to a simple chasis. We were also supplied with limited spare hardware including four infrared sensors, one ultrasonic sonar, a spare servomotor, a Motion Processing Unit (MPU) and a blue tooth module for communication with a computer or phone. To begin with, we needed to the placement of our additional hardware and perform calibrations on all the sensors. Our aim in the sensor placement was to carefully monitor the front of the robot for obstacles and to monitor a single side of the robot to ensure it remained on the correct path and to check when obstacles had been successfully avoided. Our solution tasked the robot to first find a wall in which is can oriente itself and then travel down the wall to find a corner being careful not to mistake any obstacles as walls. From there it would attempt to follow the walls and spiral towards the center of the room still being concious of obstacles. Once the 'run' was complete, the robot would stop and we would be able to view a finished map of the environment.

Electric Skateboard - Home Project

A self driven project in which I designed and built an electric skateboard. The purpose of this project was to further my engineering related abilities and to complete an entire project start to finish; something rarely seen in University projects while also producing something enjoyable to use. This projected was completed during my final semester, it was however, not university related. To begin with, a large amount of research was done into how an electric skateboard worked and the components it needed. From there I was able to determine which components best suited my aims while also ensuring all the components were able to be succesfully meshed together. The final design utilises a brushless DC motor, a Vedder ESC, two 4S 5000mAh 20C lithium polymer batteries run in series and a HK-GT2E 2-channel radio system. The board has regenerating braking and has been equipped with an anti-spark loop key which provides a low cost DIY alternative to an expensive anti-spark push-button. The finished design is highly configurable, can reach up to approximately 32 km/h, is able to run for 2 hours before needing to be recharged and is capable of going up and down hills with a respectable slope.
